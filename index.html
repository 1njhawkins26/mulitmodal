A Writer's Growth

Examining four areas of positive writing improvement over the course of English 111 and 112.

Overview

Areas, Modes, and General Improvements

While I entered English 112 already a strong writer, I undoubtedly left areas for improvement. AP Lang was excellent in laying the foundations for specific areas of writing, such as argument essays, rhetorical analysis essays, and synthesis essays, though the primary focus was on optimizing points rather than quality (which was often left as an afterthought in the form of the “Sophistication Point”). We wrote little in the way of narratives, adhered to a general outline to ensure points for rhetorical analysis and argument essays, and employed a relatively consistent, academic writing style.


Throughout English 111 and 112, I have largely been able to tame these oversights by improving in four specific areas. Structurally, I departed from strictly organizing my rhetorical analyses by device, instead focusing on bigger-picture choices such as shifts in diction and tone. I learned to write personally and emotionally with the Biography essay, overcoming my sometimes incorrect choice of clarity over nuance. I analyzed different devices from the standard set that I would choose from in AP Lang, particularly Pathos, which stemmed from my new familiarity with personal writing. And finally, I was exposed to the form of legal writing, which helped me improve at controlling my voice as a writer.


This portfolio showcases these improvements through the use of four modes: Textual, Visual, Audio, and Interactivity

Audio Analysis

The first area in which I have improved is writing rhetorical analyses, specifically in how I structure them. Previously, I was intent on organizing this style of essay by device, as it was the most straightforward for graders to comprehend. In AP Lang, the stimulus materials are provided with this in mind, though in more complex pieces, organizing by device does not fully reflect the intricacy of a work. This was the case in “Magnitudes” by Howard Nemerov, of which I wrote my analysis. In my draft, I stuck with what I was familiar with and organized it by device. However, after consulting with Mr. Liebler on the importance of tonal shifts in the poem, I decided to organize by these shifts, with the devices serving to supplement them. This proved far more effective and elevated my essay from what would have been a low A (according to Mr. Liebler) to a 100. In restructuring the essay, I found that following the poem's logic rather than imposing my own produced an analysis that the device-based approach simply could not. I have since carried this skill into English 112, employing it in my Kennedy Rhetorical Analysis Essay.

Audio Analysis

I have always thrived with writing essays built around evidence, though I have struggled with more personal writing. In English 111 last semester, I scored lowest on my personal narrative, and particularly in the Personal Qualifications/Experience section. The biography essay in English 112 allowed me to confront this weakness. My draft, however, remained somewhat impersonal, as I often described events without mentioning how Dr. Saady (My grandfather) actually felt about them. For instance, in my draft, I noted that he was confused about the circumstances of his first deployment, though I followed up with the phrase “it seemed”, immediately distancing myself from his confusion. But in my final draft, I pulled a direct quote from my interview with him regarding his confusion, and succeeded it by describing phone calls he made as a result of his emotion. This pivot gave insight into Saady as a person, as it retained clarity while overcoming the blunt descriptiveness that I previously tended to.

Audio Analysis

A product of previously employing a device-based structure in rhetorical analyses, as I mentioned in Area 1, is repeatedly using the same rhetorical devices. For me, the familiar ones were metaphors, similes, and diction leading to tone. My weakness in writing personally translated to an avoidance of analyzing Pathos, though in Kennedy’s “We Choose to Go to the Moon” speech, its use was so widespread that I simply could not avoid it. I identified how Kennedy specifically appealed to his audience at Rice by promising concrete incentives for support, and in doing so, recognized the effectiveness of Pathos when tailored to specific interests. I had avoided Pathos because it felt too broad to analyze precisely, though Kennedy’s speech forced my hand. As a result, I added another appeal to those I am comfortable working with, and I have become a more complete analytical writer.

Audio Analysis

I generally prefer to write with a stance, even if it is simply stating what tools an author uses to prove a theme in a rhetorical analysis. The Case Summary, however, demanded the opposite: a neutral overview of the Silva v. Woolworth case, free of any implicit argument. I had to remove my voice from the piece, but still select facts that would support a statement of facts for either party. The inclusion of the parenthetical noting that Silva had already moved the turkey, for example, was strategically neutral and preserved a fact central to both sides' arguments. That said, I then had to use these facts with rhetorical intent in the statement of facts, employing adverbs such as “briefly” and “immediately”, and resting my case on the very same fact of the turkey having previously been moved. Together, these legal writing assignments helped me improve by exposing me to a form of constrained writing that I had never encountered before. I can now effectively withhold or deploy my voice for appeal when necessary.
